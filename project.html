About

I build instruments that listen.

Working with custom electronics and distributed processing, I create spatial audio systems that treat the room as a sound source. Microphones capture ambient sound, movement, conversation, silence. That audio is processed through granular effects, time manipulation, and feedback circuits, then redistributed through the space. The room hears itself and responds.

My systems are portable and site-specific. Instead of bringing all equipment, I use available amplification. Each amp or speaker contributes its own character to the sonic ecology. No two installations sound the same because no two spaces are the same.

This approach emerged from years of touring, recording spaces, and processing audio through physical environments. The most interesting sounds come from letting the space participate. My role is to build the conditions for that participation.

Distributed Listener System

What if the room was the instrument?

Most spatial audio systems move sound around a space. Distributed Listener System makes the space listen to itself.

Each Listener captures a different perspective of the room, processes what it hears, and feeds that back into the space. The result is an ecology of sound that responds to the room's acoustics, the audience's presence, and what enters the space.

The system adapts to available equipment. A whisper of sound in a corner. A louder presence in front. The imperfections are what make the sound real.

This is spatial audio that works in any space. DIY venues. Galleries. Warehouses. Outdoor sites. Wherever there's sound and amplification.

How It Works

Distributed Listener System combines concepts from two previous builds. CYCL is a performance synthesizer that captures and transforms sound in real time. NODE-1 was a wall-mounted listening device for sound installations. The new system takes the processing architecture from both and distributes it wirelessly across multiple units.

Each Listener is an independent audio processor with its own microphone, custom DSP, and amplifier output. A controller transmits processing states and spatial patterns wirelessly to the Listeners. In direct mode, all Listeners mirror the same processing. In sequencer mode, processing is distributed across Listeners in different patterns at adjustable tempos, with smooth crossfading between states.

Each Listener runs custom granular algorithms for pitch shifting, time stretching, and grain manipulation. There is no central mixer. Processing is distributed across units.

The controller uses an aluminum enclosure with RF-optimized openings for wireless communication. It requires fixed power. Listener enclosures are hand-built from reclaimed and street-found materials.

Current Status

The system is in active field testing. Hardware is operational, sequencing works, wireless communication is stable. Recent tests with different amplification types confirmed that using available equipment creates richer spatial character than identical speakers.

Next developments focus on refining spatial movement patterns, building additional Listeners for multi-unit testing, and documenting placement strategies for different space types.

All development will be released as open source hardware and software.

Why It Matters

Spatial audio is often confined to specialized venues with controlled environments and expensive infrastructure. This keeps immersive sound experiences inaccessible to most artists and audiences.

Distributed Listener System breaks down those barriers by working with whatever equipment exists in a space. The system is truly portable because you only carry the Listeners and controller. Everything else is sourced locally.

By releasing all development as open source, I'm building tools that artists anywhere can adapt, modify, and expand. The goal is to make experimental spatial composition accessible beyond research centers and equipped venues.

Technology must always be in service to the music.
